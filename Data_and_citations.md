## I. Failure vs. closure

**Roughly one-third of "closed" businesses were self-reported successes by their own owners, not failures.**
*Headd, B. (2003). "Redefining Business Success: Distinguishing Between Closure and Failure." Small Business Economics, 21(1), 51–61.*

**Of Census-tracked business closures, roughly 65-70% are planned or neutral (retirement, sale of the firm, opening another firm, project completion) and roughly 25-30% are distress-driven (low sales/cash flow, credit-related).**
*U.S. Small Business Administration, Office of Advocacy, "Why Do Businesses Close?" (analysis of the Census Bureau's Annual Survey of Entrepreneurs).*

**Among 1,735 Swedish ventures tracked over 8 years, liquidation itself splits roughly evenly between firms that had performed fine and firms that hadn't — "shut down" does not reliably mean "failed."**
*Wennberg, K., Wiklund, J., DeTienne, D.R., & Cardon, M.S. (2010). "Reconceptualizing entrepreneurial exit: Divergent exit routes and their drivers." Journal of Business Venturing, 25(4), 361–375.*

**Exit types can be classified as financial harvest, stewardship, or voluntary cessation — a typology distinct from firm survival/death.**
*DeTienne, D.R., McKelvie, A., & Chandler, G.N. (2015). "Making Sense of Entrepreneurial Exit Strategies." Journal of Business Venturing, 30(2), 255–272.*

## II. Capital, VC funding, and founder outcomes

**The commonly cited "$2.6-2.9B to bring a drug to market" figure describes total company lifecycle spend, not founder-level capital at risk.**
*DiMasi, J.A., Grabowski, H.G., & Hansen, R.W. (2016). "Innovation in the pharmaceutical industry: New estimates of R&D costs." Journal of Health Economics, 47, 20–33. (Tufts Center for the Study of Drug Development)*

**Of 22,004 VC-backed companies tracked 1987-2008: 9% reached IPO, ~26% were acquired, and 34% had died after 5+ years without further funding. Roughly 75% of founders received zero income from equity even though 35% of companies reached IPO or acquisition, since many acquisitions don't clear liquidation preferences.**
*Hall, R.E., & Woodward, S.E. (2010). "The Burden of the Nondiversifiable Risk of Entrepreneurship." American Economic Review, 100(3), 1163–1194.*

**Of 212 startups studied, fewer than 25% of founder-CEOs led their company all the way to IPO; most founders surrendered management control well before that point regardless of path.**
*Wasserman, N. (2012). The Founder's Dilemmas: Anticipating and Avoiding the Pitfalls That Can Sink a Startup. Princeton University Press.*

## III. Founding teams and risk

**In a dataset of roughly 6,000 startups and up to 16,000 founders, about 65% of failures were attributable to "people problems" among the founding team — versus about 35% to product, functional, or market problems.**
*Wasserman, N. (2012). The Founder's Dilemmas: Anticipating and Avoiding the Pitfalls That Can Sink a Startup. Princeton University Press.*

**Only about 16% of U.S. tech startups in this dataset were solo-founded; the large majority build a team.**
*Wasserman, N. (2012). The Founder's Dilemmas: Anticipating and Avoiding the Pitfalls That Can Sink a Startup. Princeton University Press.*

**Across a broader (non-VC-selected) sample of 3,526 startups, teams raised more capital than solo founders, but solo founders survived longer and eventually achieved higher revenue — plausibly due to faster, less negotiated decision-making.**
*Wharton working paper, "Solo Survivors: Solo Ventures vs. Founding Teams" — ⚠️ I could not confirm the specific author names/publication venue from available sources. Verify the primary source before citing this one on a slide; treat it as directionally reliable but not yet slide-ready.*

## IV. Innovation risk frameworks (market risk vs. technical risk)

**Innovation risk has long been modeled as two independent dimensions — newness to the market and newness to the firm/technology — rather than a single risk category, producing a grid of risk profiles rather than one label per venture.**
*Booz, Allen & Hamilton (1982). New Products Management for the 1980s.*

**Corporate growth/diversification strategy has similarly been modeled along two independent axes — product newness and market newness — since the 1950s.**
*Ansoff, H.I. (1957). "Strategies for Diversification." Harvard Business Review, 35(5), 113–124.*
